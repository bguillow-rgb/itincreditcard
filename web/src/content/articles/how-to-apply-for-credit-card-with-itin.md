Applying for a credit card with an ITIN is more straightforward than most immigrants expect — the process mirrors a standard credit card application in almost every way. The real work is upfront: confirming your ITIN is valid, assembling the right documents, and targeting issuers that actually accept ITIN applicants. This guide walks you through every step so you can submit a clean application with confidence.

---

## Before I Apply — Do I Need Anything Besides My ITIN?

*A question we hear often:* Yes, and getting your document stack organized before you start will save you from getting stuck mid-application.

Here's what most ITIN-accepting issuers require at the time of application:

- **Your ITIN number** — or the original IRS assignment letter as backup
- **A valid photo ID** — a foreign passport is the strongest single document because it proves both identity and foreign status in one
- **Proof of U.S. address** — a utility bill, bank statement, or signed lease dated within 60 days
- **Proof of income** — recent pay stubs, bank statements showing regular deposits, or a prior-year tax return
- **Employer details and monthly housing cost** — standard fields on any credit application

Some issuers also request a U.S. phone number to send a one-time passcode during identity verification. A note that trips up many applicants: VoIP numbers (Google Voice, Skype) often fail bank verification systems. A physical SIM or eSIM on a major carrier is the safest option.

If you haven't gotten your ITIN yet — or if yours may have lapsed — the IRS issues ITINs through Form W-7, with standard processing taking **6 to 11 weeks** by mail. You can also apply through an IRS-authorized Certifying Acceptance Agent, who reviews your documents in person and submits on your behalf, which can reduce delays.

## How Do I Actually Fill Out the Application With an ITIN?

*This one comes up a lot:* The mechanics are nearly identical to applying with an SSN. Here's the step-by-step flow:

**Step 1 — Run a pre-qualification check.** Several ITIN-accepting issuers offer soft-pull pre-qualification. Capital One's pre-qualification tool, for example, lets you see which cards you may qualify for without affecting your credit scores. Use this before submitting any formal application.

**Step 2 — Start the online application.** When you reach the field labeled "Social Security Number," look for a dropdown or checkbox that says "ITIN" or "Other Tax Identification Number." Some forms only reveal this option after you indicate you don't have an SSN — scroll carefully before assuming the form doesn't accept one.

**Step 3 — If the online form won't accept your ITIN, call the issuer.** Most major issuers offer several application channels — online, by phone, or in person at a branch. Applying by phone with a representative often gives you more flexibility to explain your situation and have your ITIN manually entered.

**Step 4 — Enter your financial information accurately.** Income, housing cost, and employment status are used to assess your ability to repay — not just your credit history. If you're self-employed or earn income through gig work, you can include that; total annual income from all sources is what matters.

**Step 5 — Submit and wait.** Online applications often return a near-instant decision. If your application is pended for manual review, that's normal for ITIN applicants — the issuer may request additional identity documents, such as a copy of your ITIN assignment letter or a utility bill, before rendering a decision.

## What Credit Score Do I Need — And What If I Don't Have One Yet?

According to data published by Langley Federal Credit Union, in 2026 most standard unsecured credit cards require a FICO score of at least 670, while **secured cards and student cards often accept applicants with scores below 600 or no credit history at all**. Premium rewards cards typically look for scores of 740 or above.

For most ITIN holders applying for their first U.S. card, the practical answer is: you likely don't have a U.S. credit score yet, and that's fine. The card type you're targeting determines whether that's a problem:

- **Secured cards (recommended starting point):** Your deposit removes most of the issuer's risk, so many secured cards — including OpenSky and the Capital One Platinum Secured — perform no credit check at all, or approve applicants with no U.S. credit history.
- **No-deposit credit-builder cards:** Products like the Current Build Card accept ITIN holders and require no credit check and no minimum deposit, reporting your activity to all three bureaus as on-time credit usage.
- **Unsecured cards with fair credit:** Once you've built 6–12 months of payment history, cards like the Petal 2 Visa — which accepts ITIN applicants with limited credit history — become accessible.

Note that your ITIN itself doesn't change how your credit score is calculated. It simply serves as the identifier tied to your credit file. Payment history, utilization, and account age all work exactly the same way as they do for SSN holders.

## What If My Application Gets Denied?

*Readers frequently ask:* A denial with an ITIN is usually tied to one of three things: (1) the issuer doesn't accept ITINs for that specific card, (2) you couldn't verify a U.S. address, or (3) the income you reported was too low relative to the card's requirements.

If you're denied, the issuer is required by law to send you an adverse action notice within 30 days explaining the primary reason. Read it carefully — it tells you exactly what to fix. Common next steps:

- **Try a different card type.** If an unsecured card denied you, a secured card from the same issuer often has more lenient requirements.
- **Apply at a bank where you already have an account.** Issuers like Bank of America and Citi are significantly more likely to approve ITIN applicants who already have a checking or savings account with them.
- **Wait at least six months before reapplying.** Each application triggers a hard inquiry that temporarily dips your score. Spacing applications out by at least six months gives your file time to recover and strengthens your payment history.
- **Consider becoming an authorized user first.** If a trusted family member or friend with good U.S. credit adds you to their account, their positive history can begin appearing on your credit report — creating a thin-file profile that's easier for issuers to evaluate when you apply on your own.

## Does Applying Affect My Immigration Status?

No. Credit card issuers are required to report account activity to credit bureaus — not to immigration authorities. As noted across multiple legal and financial sources, ITIN holders have a legal right to apply for credit under federal Know Your Customer rules established by the Patriot Act. Submitting a credit application has no bearing on any pending or future immigration case.

That said, as of May 2026, a federal executive order directed the Treasury Department to issue guidance that may treat ITIN use as a factor requiring "enhanced due diligence" at some financial institutions. Implementation guidance is still pending as of this writing. Current accounts remain unaffected, and the order does not prohibit ITIN holders from applying for credit. Monitor communications from your bank, particularly after July 2026 when formal guidance may be issued.

## Once I'm Approved — What Should I Do Right Away?

Approval is step one. What you do in the first 30 to 90 days shapes how quickly your credit score develops.

Payment history accounts for **35% of your FICO score** — the single largest factor. Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment immediately after activation so you never accidentally miss a due date. Then keep your balance below 30% of your credit limit; on a $500 limit card, that means carrying no more than $150 at the end of each billing cycle.

Also consider diversifying your credit-building strategy early. A credit-builder loan — available from many credit unions and online lenders that accept ITINs — adds an installment account to your file alongside your credit card, which credit scoring models reward. According to data from Firstcard, building a fair credit score (580–669) typically takes 4–6 months of on-time payments from a zero baseline, while reaching a good score (670–739) usually takes 12–18 months of consistent behavior.

Once you've built that foundation, you may find yourself in a position to [transfer your ITIN credit history to a new SSN](/transfer-itin-credit-history-to-ssn) if you become eligible for one — preserving every month of history you've worked to build.
