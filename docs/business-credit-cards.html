Quick Answer

ITIN holders who run a business, even as a sole proprietor, can apply for business credit cards. Many issuers accept an ITIN plus an EIN (Employer Identification Number), which you can get free from the IRS in minutes. Business cards offer higher limits, business-focused rewards, and help build a commercial credit profile that operates independently of your personal score.

✓ Checked against: IRS ·CFPB ·Issuer disclosures

Who can get a business credit card with an ITIN?

Business credit cards are not limited to large companies. Sole proprietors, freelancers, gig workers, small business owners operating under their own name, qualify. If you have an ITIN and any self-employment income (whether from a formal business, freelance work, rideshare driving, or a side business), you have the profile for a business card application.

The application will ask for your business name (your personal name is fine for a sole prop), your federal tax ID (ITIN or EIN), and basic business information: type of business, annual revenue, and number of employees (zero or one is fine). Many issuers also require a personal guarantee from the owner.

ITIN vs. EIN, which do you need for a business card?

Some business card issuers accept an ITIN as the business owner's personal tax ID. Others require an EIN, an Employer Identification Number, as the business's tax ID. Here's a clear breakdown:

Tax ID What it is How to get it When needed for business cards
ITIN Your personal tax ID if you don't have an SSN IRS Form W-7; takes 7-11 weeks Used as owner's personal identifier in the application
EIN Your business's tax ID, separate from personal Free at IRS.gov; issued instantly online Required by some issuers; also starts a separate business credit file

Getting an EIN is quick and free, even if you're a sole proprietor with no employees. Go to IRS.gov and use the EIN online application. You'll receive the EIN immediately. Having one expands your business card options and begins a commercial credit history.

Will a business card check or build my personal credit?

This trips up most first-time applicants, so be precise about it. Nearly every small-business card requires a personal guarantee, which means the issuer pulls your personal credit (a hard inquiry) and you are personally liable for the debt even though the account is "the business's." For ITIN holders that is actually useful: your personal file is what you are trying to build, and the application leans on it. The split happens on reporting, not on the application. Three patterns exist:

  • Reports only to commercial bureaus (the most common for major issuers). Day-to-day balances stay off your personal report, which protects your personal utilization, but you only build business credit. A late payment can still be reported to your personal file.
  • Reports to both. Smaller issuers, credit unions, and several fintech business cards report activity to the consumer bureaus too, so the account builds personal and business credit at once. For an ITIN holder with a thin file, this is the more valuable structure.
  • Reports to neither in a useful way. A few cards report nothing to commercial bureaus unless you go delinquent. Verify the reporting policy in writing before you apply, because it determines whether the card builds anything at all.

Decision rule: if your goal is a stronger personal FICO score, choose a card that reports to the consumer bureaus and keep your reported balance under 30% of the limit, the "amounts owed" category is 30% of a FICO score (FICO). If your goal is a credit line that does not drag down your personal utilization, choose a commercial-only reporter and treat the personal guarantee as the cost of entry.

How to build business credit with an ITIN

Business credit is scored separately from personal credit, on its own scale. A Dun & Bradstreet PAYDEX score runs 0-100 (not the 300-850 range of a FICO score), and 80 or above signals consistently on-time payment to vendors and lenders (Dun & Bradstreet). The major commercial bureaus, Dun & Bradstreet (D&B), Experian Business, and Equifax Business, each maintain their own business profiles. To build yours:

  1. Get an EIN from the IRS. Free, instant, done in 10 minutes at IRS.gov. This is your business's tax identity.
  2. Open a business bank account using your EIN and ITIN. Separation of business finances is important for both credit and taxes.
  3. Apply for a business credit card at an ITIN-accepting issuer. Use the card for business expenses and pay it on time.
  4. Register with D&B (Dun & Bradstreet). Claim or create your business's D-U-N-S number for free at dnb.com. This enables commercial credit reporting.
  5. Open trade lines with vendors. Net-30 accounts with vendors who report to commercial bureaus (office supply companies, wholesale distributors) build business credit fastest.

Should you get a business card now, or wait?

A business card is the right next move only at a specific stage. Use this framework before you apply:

  • Wait if your personal credit is still thin. Because most business cards require a personal guarantee and pull your personal credit, applying with no score and no history usually means a denial and a wasted hard inquiry, which can shave a few points and stays on your report for two years (CFPB). Build six to twelve months on a secured personal card first.
  • Apply if you have real business expenses and a usable score. Once you have a FICO score in roughly the 640+ range and recurring business spending, a business card separates those expenses cleanly, which the IRS expects you to be able to substantiate for deductions, and starts a commercial file.
  • Get the EIN regardless. It is free and instant, opens more issuer options, and lets you keep your ITIN off some applications.

Mistakes ITIN business owners make

  • Mixing personal and business spending on one card. It muddies your tax records and forfeits the cleanest benefit of a business card.
  • Assuming the card builds personal credit. Many report to commercial bureaus only, confirm the reporting policy first (see the section above).
  • Carrying a balance for "rewards." Business-card APRs commonly run well into the twenties; interest erases any cash-back or points value. Pay in full.
  • Skipping the D-U-N-S number. Without it, vendor trade lines may have nowhere to report, so the business file never starts.

Common questions

Can I get a business credit card with an ITIN and no SSN?

Yes. Sole proprietors and some small business owners can apply for a business credit card using an ITIN as their personal tax ID and/or an EIN as the business tax ID. Not every issuer accepts this combination, but many credit unions and several national issuers do. Always confirm before applying.

Do I need a registered business to get a business credit card?

Not necessarily. Sole proprietors, people running a business as themselves, without a formal LLC or corporation, can typically apply for a business card. You'll need to provide your business name (which can be your own name), your ITIN, and your EIN if you have one.

What is an EIN and do I need one?

An Employer Identification Number (EIN) is a nine-digit business tax ID issued by the IRS. You can get one for free in minutes at IRS.gov, even if you're a sole proprietor with no employees. Some business card issuers require an EIN; others accept an ITIN alone. Having an EIN also helps build a separate business credit profile.

Does a business credit card affect my personal credit?

It depends on the issuer. Some business cards report to personal credit bureaus (which means both your balance and payments affect your personal score); others report only to commercial bureaus like Dun & Bradstreet. Check the issuer's reporting policy before applying.

What are the advantages of a business credit card over a personal card?

Business cards typically offer higher credit limits, rewards tuned to business spending (advertising, office supplies, travel, dining), and cleaner separation between personal and business expenses, which simplifies taxes. They also begin building a business credit history, which is separate from your personal FICO score.
