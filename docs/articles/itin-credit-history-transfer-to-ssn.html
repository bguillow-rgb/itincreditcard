ITIN Credit History: How to Transfer It to Your New SSN

By Sofía Castillo · Secured & Starter Cards Writer · Published June 18, 2026

Advertiser disclosure:ITIN Credit Card is an independent resource. Some links on this page are from partners who compensate us, which may affect which products we feature and where. This does not change our editorial guidance and is never a cost to you. See our full disclosure.

Quick Answer

Your ITIN credit history does NOT transfer to your new SSN automatically. You must notify the IRS first, then write to all three credit bureaus (Experian, TransUnion, Equifax) requesting a merge. You also need to update your ITIN on file with every credit card issuer. Done correctly, the transfer takes 30-60 days and your credit score stays intact.

You spent months, maybe years, using a credit card with your ITIN to build a U.S. credit history from zero. Then the day comes: you receive a Social Security number. Good news, right? Yes, but there is one task you have to complete quickly, or all that credit-building work may effectively disappear from your new identity. This guide explains every step of the process, including what to do with your existing credit cards.

Will I lose my credit history when I get an SSN?

A question we hear often:

No, but only if you act. When you are assigned a new Social Security number, your previous credit history will not automatically transfer. The credit bureaus have no way of knowing you received an SSN unless you tell them. You can have a credit history with an ITIN, but once you get a Social Security number, you must notify the credit bureaus to move your history under your new SSN.

Here is the reassuring part: the date your credit history began is static. It will not change if the bank identifies you with your Social Security number instead of your ITIN. Every on-time payment you made on your ITIN credit card, every month of low utilization, the full age of your account, all of it carries over once the merge is complete. There is no reset button.

Credit bureaus (Experian, TransUnion, and Equifax) can create and maintain credit files using an ITIN. When you open a credit account with an ITIN and make payments, the lender reports that activity to the credit bureaus, just like they would for someone with an SSN. Over time, you build a credit history, and eventually, you get a credit score. That file is what you are protecting by completing the transfer correctly.

What is the step-by-step process to transfer my ITIN credit file to my SSN?

There are three distinct stages. Skipping any one of them creates gaps that can hurt you later.

Stage 1: Notify the IRS

Once you receive an SSN, you must use that number for tax purposes and discontinue using your ITIN. The IRS does not do this automatically. It is your responsibility to notify the IRS so they can combine all of your tax records under one identification number. If you do not notify the IRS when you are assigned an SSN, you may not receive credit for all wages paid and taxes withheld.

You can visit a local IRS office or write a letter explaining that you have now been assigned an SSN and want your tax records combined. Include your complete name, mailing address, and ITIN along with a copy of your social security card and a copy of the CP 565, Notice of ITIN Assignment, if available. After processing, the IRS will void the ITIN and associate all prior tax information filed under the ITIN with the SSN.

Stage 2: Transfer your credit file at all three bureaus

Wait until you receive written confirmation from the IRS before doing this step. Then write to each credit bureau requesting that your credit history associated with your ITIN be linked to your new SSN, including a copy of the IRS confirmation letter along with copies of both your ITIN and SSN.

Mail your requests to:

  • Equifax: P.O. Box 740241, Atlanta, GA 30374
  • Experian: P.O. Box 2002, Allen, TX 75013
  • TransUnion: P.O. Box 1000, Chester, PA 19022

The transfer should not take more than 30-60 days and should not affect your credit scores.

Stage 3: Update your credit card issuers

Updating the bureaus does not automatically update your individual card accounts. For credit card companies, call the customer service line to ask about the process. Have your new SSN, old ITIN, and account number ready. Ask the representative to update your profile and confirm that future payment reporting will go to your SSN-linked file. This prevents a split-file problem where some payments report to one identifier and others to a different one.

What happens to my credit cards while the transfer is in progress?

This one comes up a lot:

Your cards keep working normally. Receiving an SSN does not close, freeze, or alter any of your existing credit card accounts. You can keep swiping, keep earning rewards, and keep making payments as usual throughout the entire transfer process. The only thing changing in the background is which identification number your lender and the bureaus attach to your account records.

That said, do not delay starting the process. The use of an ITIN does not prevent individuals from developing credit scores. Credit bureaus track credit activity associated with ITINs in the same manner as they do for SSNs. But once you have an SSN, lenders may begin pulling your SSN-based file for future decisions. If that file shows no history because the merge has not happened yet, you could face unexpected denials or worse terms on new applications even though you have a solid payment record.

Does the same process apply if I want to apply for a new credit card after getting my SSN?

Readers frequently ask:

Yes, with one important wrinkle. Once the credit bureau merge is complete, applying for a new credit card with your SSN will pull your merged credit file, which will reflect the full history you built with your ITIN cards. That is the ideal outcome.

If you apply for a new card before the merge is finished, the issuer may pull an empty SSN file and see you as someone with no credit history at all. That would likely result in denial or a low-limit secured card offer, even though you may have 12-24 months of strong ITIN credit card history sitting in a separate file. Complete all three stages of the transfer and confirm with at least one bureau that your file shows your full history before submitting any new credit card application.

Once your merged file is in place, you become eligible for a much broader set of cards. Issuers that previously required an SSN become accessible, and the rewards cards that were out of reach when you were just starting out may now be within your approval range. See our guide to rewards credit cards for ITIN holders for a sense of what opens up as your profile strengthens.

How does the ITIN-to-SSN transfer affect my actual credit score?

Done correctly, the transfer has no negative effect. The use of an ITIN does not prevent individuals from developing credit scores. Credit bureaus track credit activity associated with ITINs in the same manner as they do for SSNs. Because the scoring math is identical, a merged file with a clean payment history produces the same score it would have produced under the ITIN.

The mechanics of credit scoring are the same whether you use an ITIN or an SSN. Your ITIN does not change how your score is calculated. What matters is the behavior on the account: payment history, utilization, account age, and the mix of credit types you carry. Those factors are all preserved in the transfer.

According to CNBC Select, if you are starting from scratch, it will generally take between two to six months of credit activity to establish a credit score. The good news for ITIN holders doing the transfer is that they are not starting from scratch. They are simply moving an existing score to a new identifier.

What if I had a bad experience and want to start fresh with my SSN instead?

Starting fresh with a new SSN file sounds appealing if your ITIN credit history includes late payments or a high-utilization period. But there are two serious problems with attempting this.

First, negative items associated with your ITIN may still surface. Lenders and bureaus increasingly cross-reference names, addresses, and dates of birth. A negative ITIN-linked tradeline may be connected to your SSN file even without a formal transfer request. Second, choosing not to transfer means abandoning all your positive history as well: every on-time payment, the full age of your oldest account, and any low-utilization months that boosted your score. You would be left with an empty SSN file and would need another two to six months just to generate a score at all.

If your ITIN credit history has serious negative marks, the better path is to consult a nonprofit credit counselor before deciding. The National Foundation for Credit Counseling (NFCC) offers free or low-cost guidance. For most ITIN holders with a clean or mixed record, transferring and then focusing on building forward is the stronger strategy.

What documents do I need to complete the credit transfer?

Gather these before you start:

  • Your IRS confirmation letter showing your ITIN has been voided and linked to your SSN (CP 565 if available)
  • A copy of your new Social Security card
  • A copy of your ITIN assignment notice
  • Proof of identity (passport or government-issued photo ID)
  • Your current mailing address, ideally supported by a utility bill or lease

Individuals who do not yet have an ITIN can apply through the IRS using Form W-7. This process requires proof of identity and foreign status documentation, and typically takes 7-11 weeks to complete. Common documentation includes a valid ITIN confirmation letter from the IRS, government-issued photo identification such as a passport or foreign driver’s license, proof of residence such as utility bills or lease agreements, and proof of income such as pay stubs, bank statements, or tax returns. Many of these same documents are what you will reference when writing to the bureaus.

If you have not yet applied for an ITIN and are still building toward your first U.S. credit card, our guide to how to apply for a credit card with an ITIN covers the starting point in detail. The bottom line: the transfer process is straightforward and the timeline is manageable. The only wrong move is doing nothing and hoping the merge happens on its own.

Common questions

Will I lose my credit score when I get a Social Security number?

No, not if you complete the transfer correctly. Your credit score is tied to your credit file, not your ITIN or SSN number. Once the three credit bureaus merge your ITIN file with your new SSN, your full history, including every on-time payment from your ITIN credit cards, carries over. The date your credit history began does not reset.

Does the ITIN-to-SSN credit history transfer happen automatically?

No. The transfer is not automatic. When you receive an SSN, the credit bureaus do not receive a notification. You have to take action yourself: first notify the IRS, wait for their confirmation letter, then send that letter along with copies of both your ITIN and SSN to each of the three major credit bureaus by mail.

How long does it take to transfer ITIN credit history to a new SSN?

According to CreditCards.com, the transfer at the credit bureau level should take no more than 30-60 days after you send your written requests. Allow extra time for the IRS step, which comes first. In total, budget about 60-90 days from the day you receive your SSN to the day your merged credit file is confirmed.

Do I need to update my ITIN on my credit cards after getting an SSN?

Yes. Updating the credit bureaus does not automatically update your card issuers. Call or write each issuer separately and ask them to replace your ITIN with your new SSN on your account. This matters because future payment reporting needs to go to your SSN-linked file rather than creating a duplicate record.

Can I still use my ITIN credit cards after I receive my SSN?

Yes, the cards themselves remain open and usable. Receiving an SSN does not cancel or freeze any existing credit card account. The only thing that changes is the identification number linked to those accounts. Your spending power, credit limit, and rewards are completely unaffected during and after the transfer process.

What if I built bad credit with my ITIN? Should I still transfer the history?

This depends on how negative the history is. Transferring a strong ITIN credit file to your SSN is clearly beneficial. If your ITIN history has serious negative marks, some financial advisors suggest consulting a credit counselor before merging, since those negatives will follow you into your SSN file. Starting fresh is not recommended without professional guidance.
