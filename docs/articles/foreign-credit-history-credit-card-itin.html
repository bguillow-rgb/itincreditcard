Use Foreign Credit History for a U.S. Credit Card With ITIN

By Editorial Staff · Secured & Starter Cards · Published July 15, 2026

Quick Answer

Yes, in limited ways. American Express's Global Card Relationship lets existing Amex cardholders apply for a U.S. card using a passport or ITIN. Nova Credit translates foreign bureau data for select issuers, but typically needs an ITIN. Most ITIN holders still need a secured card to build a U.S. credit file from scratch.

You spent years building a solid credit record back home, paying bills on time, holding cards responsibly, maybe even reaching an excellent score in your home country’s system. Then you arrived in the United States with an ITIN and discovered that none of it automatically counts here. It’s a frustrating reality that catches a lot of newcomers off guard, but it’s not a dead end. A handful of real pathways exist to put what you already built to work, and this guide covers every one of them.

Does my foreign credit history count for anything when I apply for a U.S. credit card with an ITIN?

The three major U.S. credit bureaus do not import foreign credit files. Your score back home does not appear in the U.S. system automatically, even if the bureau shares a name with one you recognize. That applies whether your home-country file is with an Experian affiliate, a local Equifax bureau, or anyone else.

That said, your foreign history isn’t entirely useless. A small number of lenders use services like Nova Credit to pull foreign data voluntarily, but this isn’t a transfer. It’s better described as a translation: a third-party service converts your foreign bureau data into a format that participating U.S. lenders can read during underwriting. If you already hold a card with American Express in your home country, you may also qualify for a U.S. card through their Global Card Relationship program, using that existing relationship as a proxy for creditworthiness. Neither route is available at every issuer, but both are real, documented, and used by ITIN holders today.

What is Nova Credit and how does it work for ITIN holders?

A question we hear often: many readers arrive knowing their home-country bureau score but not understanding how it can cross the border.

Nova Credit is a financial technology company that partners with credit bureaus in select countries and translates a foreign credit file into what they call a Credit Passport. A participating U.S. lender then reads that Credit Passport as supplemental underwriting data during the application review. Nova Credit can translate credit history from countries including India, the UK, Canada, Mexico, and others into a format some U.S. lenders accept. The company has expanded its supported country list over time, so it’s worth checking their current coverage directly.

One important detail for ITIN holders specifically: as of 2025, Nova Credit’s Credit Passport process generally requires an SSN or ITIN first. That means Nova Credit is most useful for ITIN holders who already have their number in hand, not for people still waiting on one. If you have your ITIN, bring it to the Nova Credit application so any approved card gets tied to your ITIN from the start and will report to U.S. bureaus.

How does the American Express Global Card Relationship work with an ITIN?

This one comes up a lot, especially among immigrants who held Amex cards abroad before relocating.

American Express operates a program (formally called Global Card Relationship, commonly called Global Transfer) that lets an existing cardholder apply for a U.S. Amex card using their international account history instead of a U.S. credit file. To be eligible, your existing card must be issued by American Express, you must be the primary cardholder, and you must have held the card for at least three months, with the account open and in good standing at the time you apply. In practice, the credit community generally recommends six to twelve months of history for better odds, though Amex’s published minimum stays at three months.

Amex accepts a U.S. home address, U.S. home phone number, U.S. bank account details, and either a passport, U.S.-issued SSN, or U.S.-issued ITIN for the Global Card Relationship application. That flexibility is what makes this path appealing: you don’t need a U.S. credit file, and a passport alone can satisfy the identity requirement if you don’t yet have an ITIN.

Here is the critical catch that most guides miss. A U.S. card opened through Global Transfer reports to American Express only, not to the three U.S. credit bureaus, and you cannot add an ITIN to that card later. So if you apply without an ITIN, you get a real Amex card and the rewards that come with it, but you don’t build an Experian, TransUnion, or Equifax credit file. What you can do after getting your ITIN is apply for a second U.S. Amex card and attach your ITIN in the SSN field on that application. That second card will report to all three bureaus and begin building your formal U.S. credit score. Most people who go this route end up using both cards together: the first for rewards, the second to establish bureau history.

According to a 2026 Experian white paper, ITIN holders have a lower debt-to-income ratio and fewer accounts past due compared to SSN holders, and once an immigrant enters the credit system, they carry credit scores 26.7 points higher on average by age 30 compared to non-immigrants the same age living in the same area. That matters because it means lenders who evaluate ITIN applications manually tend to see strong outcomes, which is part of why some community lenders are willing to review translated foreign credit reports at all.

Can a community bank or credit union review my foreign credit report manually?

Readers frequently ask whether they can simply walk into a branch and show their home-country credit report.

The answer is: sometimes, and it’s worth trying before you rule it out. A few credit unions and community banks will accept a paper or PDF copy of your foreign credit report as part of a manual review. You walk in with a translated CIBIL, Experian UK, or SCHUFA report, and a loan officer evaluates you case by case. This is not a standardized process, and approval depends on the officer, the bank, and the product. Secured cards in particular are the product most likely to be reviewed this way, because the deposit reduces the lender’s risk regardless of your background.

If you want to try this route, call the lending department before you go in. Ask specifically whether they accept translated foreign credit reports as supplemental documentation for credit card applications. A quick call saves you from wasting a hard pull on a branch that can’t process foreign reports. Bring your ITIN letter, a certified translation of your foreign report, your passport, and proof of a U.S. address. Credit unions that serve immigrant communities are often more experienced with this kind of manual underwriting than large national banks.

What documents should I bring when applying through any of these programs?

This one comes up a lot in our inbox, usually a few days before someone’s appointment.

The exact document list varies by program, but the following covers the core of what all three pathways require in some form:

  • ITIN letter from the IRS (keep the original; issuers want to see it)
  • Valid passport (foreign government-issued, unexpired)
  • Proof of U.S. address: a utility bill, lease agreement, or bank statement in your name
  • Proof of income: recent pay stubs, an offer letter, or tax returns
  • U.S. bank account details: routing and account number, needed to fund a secured deposit or pay your card
  • For Nova Credit: access to your home-country bureau account to authorize the data pull
  • For Amex Global Transfer: your existing non-U.S. Amex account number and the card itself

The ITIN application process requires proof of identity and foreign status documentation and typically takes 7-11 weeks to complete. If you don’t yet have your ITIN, plan around that timeline before your card application, because attaching your ITIN at the moment of application is the only way to ensure a new card reports to U.S. bureaus from day one. What should I do if none of these paths work for my situation?

For many ITIN holders, particularly those from countries not yet supported by Nova Credit and those without an existing Amex relationship, the most reliable path is still a U.S. secured credit card opened directly with an ITIN. The OpenSky Secured Credit Card is one of the few secured cards that openly approves applicants without an SSN: an ITIN works, there is no credit check during application, and OpenSky reports your account activity to all three major credit bureaus every month.

Most ITIN-friendly cards start with a credit limit between $200 and $700, and limits often increase after 6-12 months of on-time payments. After about a year of responsible use, you may qualify for unsecured cards with higher limits and rewards. Starting from zero is slower than putting existing history to work, but the destination is the same: a bureau-tracked U.S. credit file that opens doors to better cards, higher limits, and lower rates over time. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use my credit history from another country to get approved for a U.S. credit card with an ITIN? Yes, but only through specific programs. American Express’s Global Card Relationship accepts existing Amex cardholders using a passport or ITIN. Nova Credit translates bureau data from supported countries for a small number of U.S. issuers. Outside those programs, U.S. lenders do not automatically see your foreign credit file.

Does the American Express Global Card Relationship require an ITIN? No. Amex officially accepts a passport, U.S. SSN, or ITIN for the Global Card Relationship application. You can be approved with just a passport, but a card opened without an ITIN does not report to the three U.S. credit bureaus, only to American Express internally.

What countries does Nova Credit support for U.S. credit card applications? As of mid-2026, Nova Credit supports credit history translation from countries including India, Mexico, Canada, the UK, Australia, Brazil, the Dominican Republic, Kenya, Nigeria, the Philippines, and Switzerland, among others. Supported countries and participating U.S. issuers change, so check Nova Credit’s site directly before applying.

Will a U.S. credit card opened through Amex Global Transfer build my U.S. credit score if I have no ITIN? No. A U.S. Amex card opened without an ITIN builds an internal history with American Express only. It does not report to Experian, TransUnion, or Equifax. To build a bureau-tracked U.S. credit score, you need an ITIN attached to a card that reports to all three bureaus.

What is the fastest way for an ITIN holder with foreign credit to start building a U.S. credit score? Open a U.S. secured credit card using your ITIN. Cards like the OpenSky Secured Visa or Capital One Platinum Secured accept ITINs and report to all three major bureaus monthly. Pair that with an Amex Global Transfer card (if eligible) for access to better rewards while your bureau file grows.
